Welcome

Industrial symbiosis is the simple idea that that waste products of one industry could become the feed stocks for another industry.

A "waste product" doesn’t have to be something that goes into a landfill - although it might be. Other valuable waste products are all around us. See a plume of steam escaping from a building? That’s waste heat and water - that might be quite useful for another process that heats things up. Or left-overs from field crops that might be considered useless - may become quite useful if directed towards other technologies that can use it. Even sunlight that falls for free all around us could be used to create electricity or charge batteries.

Not every idea makes sense in every situation. But sometimes taking a broader look is all that is needed to start down a new and interesting pathway to economic development.

Industrial symbiosis involves public or private partners working together to make the most productive use of material, water, and energy streams—improving the economic savings for each partner while reducing combined environmental impact and often eliminating many expenses.

The City of Kalundborg in Denmark was one of the first places in the world to demonstrate the effectiveness of industrial symbiosis on a large scale. Today this partnership results in these combined benefits each year:

  • Bottom-line savings of more than $24 million USD
  • An additional $15.5 million USD savings in socio-economic benefits for local residents
  • Resource savings that include: 635,000 metric tons of CO2, nearly 1 billion gallons of water, 100 GWh of energy, and 87,000 tons of materials that are cycled back into productive use.

(Source: Ellen MacArthur Foundation -- https://www.ellenmacarthurfoundation.org/case-studies/effective-industrial-symbiosis)

According to research funded by the European Union that examines conditions required to implement an industrial symbiosis collaboration, important considerations include:

  1. Stakeholder cooperation – Strong social networks between partners; trust and openness to working together.
  2. Working with Intermediaries / knowledge brokers – Coordinators with expertise establishing partnerships can facilitate communication and cooperation between different interest groups. These can include local/state government actors or NGOs
  3. Mapping geography / resource flows – Place-based projects that establish industrial symbiosis in particular communities require inventorying recoverable streams (of waste energy, heat, CO2, water, materials, etc.) and assessing potentially beneficial direct resource-sharing relationships between partners.

(Source: Scaler Project, Lessons Learnt and Best Practices for Enhancing Industrial Symbiosis in the Process Industry. https://www.scalerproject.eu/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/Report-Industrial-Symbiosis-Best-Practices-H2020-SCALER.pdf)

Clearly there are many factors to consider when implementing a collaborative resource-sharing project! The guide presented here is meant to assist with consideration #3, helping you to specifically identify resource flows so you can begin mapping the relationships between potential partners. Identifying resource flows can be an iterative process that may require some exploration and time spent clicking through multiple pathways. The result of this exploration won’t be a single defined answer, but you will learn how to spot potential "symbioses" (resource-sharing relationships) and you will learn about technologies that you may want to consider investing in that will help you to unlock new value-generating relationships.

The most straightforward way to kickstart an industrial symbiosis is by establishing an "anchor" partner. This partner usually represents a large and significant industry that will generate multiple, valuable waste streams that other industries can tap into.

The resource guide presented here can be tailored to examine resource relationships through the lens of many different anchor industries, however, version 1.0 explores opportunities specifically within the forestry industry. This industry is a jumping off point due to interest from the Washington State Legislature and Department of Natural Resources in helping the Port of Willapa in Pacific County, Washington to establish an energy innovation district built around forestry-based resources.

These wood shavings don't have to go to a landfill, they can be repurposed and potentially make a profit.

How the guide works

This Industrial Symbiosis online guide is designed to help businesses, local economic development experts, government officials, planners, and natural resource business owners to take a broader look and identify and compare possible alternative uses for waste products. We use forestry-related wastes and byproducts to offer a focused example.

What the guide can't do

In order to determine whether promising opportunities are feasible, users will need detailed information about their local markets in order to do effective and complete cost-benefit analyses about industrial symbiosis opportunities. This guide cannot perform calculations, but is designed to provide enough information and direction for users to begin developing a compelling business plan.

Significant timber biomass in the Gifford Pinchot National Forest, near Stevenson, Washington, was frequently burned instead of used as a source of renewable energy. In 2012, the idea for the Wind River Biomass Utility (WRBU) was born as a way to repurpose that biomass for a combined heat and power utility. The WRBU purchased an ORC generator and gasification unit that transform the timber byproduct into electrical power and CO2, heat and biochar. In the future, the WRBU endeavors to purchase waste wood from forest management operations in addition to milling residuals—a total of 12,000 dry tons of waste wood. With that amount of waste wood, the ORC generator could produce nearly 4 million kW per year for a variety of business and residential used in the Wind River Business Park. With the gasification unit, the operation also aims to produce 40 million BTU of thermal power and 1,200 tons of biochar per year. Visit the Port of Skamania site to learn more about the Wind River project, read the business plan and understand more about their research.

Next steps

There are many resources available in starting a business that should be consulted to help a new business enterprise to get launched. While beyond the reach of this model, a few key factors are noted below.

The "do nothing" baseline

When embarking on any industrial symbiotic endeavor use the "do nothing" scenario as a point of comparison with a broad range of alternatives. The Center for Sustainable Infrastructure recommends looking for alternatives to business as usual that have "the same or better levels of service for the same or less cost." All potential opportunities should be compared to the current byproduct disposal cost, otherwise referred to as the "do nothing" scenario. Opportunities for industrial symbiosis almost always require additional labor, equipment and/or energy to actualize, yet as long as the net difference between the "do nothing" and symbiotic options is "the same or better," then the industrial symbiosis project may be worth pursuing.

Access to capital and financing can be a significant barrier to implementing industrial symbiotic processes in rural areas where the potential is greatest. There are a number of options to explore around equity, raising capital, securing debt instruments and other financial strategies when creating an industrial symbiotic relationship.

Private capital/ debt

If debt is part of the picture, many lenders want the advocate to also have "skin in the game" with their own capital resources as equity.

Private loans typically have equity requirements, wherein the lender holds a stake in a business’ equity in exchange for additional capital. Unless independently wealthy, it is a good idea to seek additional funding when creating a new line of business. It’s a vital way to demonstrate that concept has attracted multiple investors and it shows diversification in business practice, which is attractive to lenders.

Reaching out to other partners

Industrial symbiosis is seldom a single endeavor as it requires coordination between suppliers and off-takers. Existing businesses with waste products may have an incentive to work with and invest in a company that can turn their waste into profitable products.

An effective way to secure additional financing is to consult current or prior business partners to see if any are interested in forming an industrial symbiotic partnership. Having a track record of other business successes is a helpful way to encourage partnerships.

Washington State Department of Ecology grants

The Department of Ecology offers grants for a variety of environmental remediation, resource management and climate protection initiatives. Explore these grants to see if any of the offerings apply to your preferred industrial symbiotic process.

Public Port Districts

Washington State has unique economic development engines, public port districts, that help spur innovation, investment and job creation statewide. There are 75 separate port districts throughout the state, ranging from traditional seaports and airports to river ports, community marinas and landlocked, industrial areas. Port districts function similarly to government entities in that they collect taxes and revenues but differ in their ability to turn a profit on their investments, so long as those profits are reinvested into their respective districts. Port districts prioritize job creation and many offer below-market-value rent, incubator space, innovation hubs and more. It’s worth investigating which port district serves your area and contacting a representative to see whether investment in your industrial symbiotic process is possible and/or if there is vacant, port-owned land you could rent to store treatment technology or waste products, among other opportunities.

University and educational partners

University and educational partners can offer a variety of free or discounted assistance as well as fuller partnership agreements. For example, Masters or PhD student capstone projects can be a mutually beneficial opportunity to connect educational innovation with real-world business cases.

One such example of a successful university-private partnership is the South Landing Hub Facility and Central Energy Plant in Spokane, a partnership between Avista, McKinstry, Gonzaga and the University of Washington. This partnership is building one of the largest zero-energy buildings in the world. The building features 40,000 square feet of mixed-use office and commercial space, as well as a central energy plant to power the Hub building as well as the neighboring building. Sharing energy resources across multiple buildings allows for a living laboratory of energy sharing potential that all partners are interested in opportunities to replicate across the built environment.

Operational

Many businesses tend to overvalue the risk of ownership and operation if local experience is limited, however, operational needs for symbiotic systems are not necessarily unusual.

Industrial symbiotic system management

It takes creativity and gumption to launch a new industrial symbiotic process, particularly for the "use in another external process" options. It requires maintaining belief overcoming obstacles that there is a better way. This mixture of persistence and creativity is necessary as few innovations find easy entry into everyday use. Fortunately, it’s a big world, and many of the most promising symbiotic technologies are already being deployed somewhere. For example, two principals of the went to Europe to visit renewable energy-powered greenhouses.

Mechanics of product delivery

The mechanics of product delivery vary depending on the product and system, but the mechanics of a district energy system can serve as one example:

  • Hire a manager with good math skills to manage billing, maintenance requests, neighborhood/customer relations.
  • Hire a planning/permitting firm to navigate getting permissions and/or easements for pipe network for new extensions of the system.
  • Secure a lawyer to draw up contracts between supplier and customers.
  • Hire a mechanical engineering firm to design the systems.
  • Bring on a less expensive mechanical engineering firm for on-going maintenance. They maintain the pumps, monitor system operations (which has electronic monitoring equipment - akin to smart building systems)
  • Hire a contractor to install the hardware.
  • Rent a desk and a phone!

Life expectancy of project inputs

Projects are built with capital and operations and maintenance (O+M) budgets. In order to prepare an adequate O+M budget, it’s important to know the life expectancy of each of the elements, such as technology and infrastructure delivery systems. For example, pumps may have a ten-year lifecycle before replacement is necessary, while pipes can be operational for anywhere between fifty to one hundred years. A successful budget accounts for these lifecycles, including funds for regular maintenance of parts.

This is especially important when comparing alternatives to the no action base case. Sometimes, a part that wears out often will make more economic sense than buying a part that never wears out. It just depends on the cost of replacement and the life span that can be expected of these parts.

Market variability and resilience to extraordinary events

Producers of natural resource products are not strangers to market variability due to commodity pricing swings. Industrial symbiotic products may also have seasonal or other market cycles that will be important to understand thoroughly when exploring industrial symbiosis opportunities.

Additionally, nearly 40% of small businesses don’t recover from extraordinary events, like natural disasters. Building financial runway to ensure resiliency during unexpected events is vital to sustaining spinoff symbiotic business endeavors.

Overcoming barriers to access technology

Some technologies are easy to implement locally, such as a wood chipper to create bark, while other, more significant investments—like a wood composite facility—require larger capital, debt, and financial strategies.

Addressing knowledge and experience gaps

More complex technologies may have a learning curve for operators, particularly in learning about quantity and quality of byproduct inputs. One of the best ways to bridge this information gap is to speak with others who are already using specific technologies or implemented a similar industrial symbiotic process. The can help connect you with resources for knowledge sharing.

Another viable option is to connect employees with additional training programs. See the "Workforce and training" section.

Transportation and storage of byproducts

Transportation and storage of byproducts

The majority of byproducts require some particular treatment to transform a former waste product into a viable option for internal reuse, input to another external process, or direct to market application. In this application, treatment can be something as simple as a compost pile, or more complex, like an anaerobic digester.

When considering the viability of an industrial symbiotic opportunity, it is important to consider whether a centralized or distributed technology setup is more advantageous. Centralized setups have a single or a few locations for a form of technology, which requires users to transport byproducts to one location. Conversely, distributed systems render the technology mobile, where the technology itself is transported to the location of the byproducts. In some cases, distributed systems are more resilient because when disaster strikes, it may knock out only a portion of the system capacity if that capacity is spread over multiple sites or pieces of equipment. A centralized options–-where all the eggs are in one basket—can be attractive in terms of scale and efficiency, but be less resilient because it only takes the one big machine to fail for the whole system to fail.

It is also important to understand the amount of space needed to store byproduct feedstock as it accumulates, especially because in many cases, a specific volume is necessary before a technological or market application is viable. Here are some questions to answer as you evaluate the potential of an industrial symbiotic process:

  • How much of the is potential stock is produced and available from the typical lifecycle activity (e.g. logging)?
  • How much of the waste product is required for the identified technological treatment process or direct to market option?
  • Where is the technology located? How frequently would the former waste product need to be transported there?
  • Are there nearby operations that would be interested in technology sharing?

Marketing begins with effectively telling the story of the innovation or product. How it is branded and positioned in the market is the subject of an overall communications plan. Communications plans often identify target audiences such as customers, investors, supply chain partners, and employees, and then specifies the messages that is most beneficial to communicate with each.

Opportunity Zones

In 2017, the Federal Government instituted tax incentives aimed at encouraging investors to fund businesses in underserved communities. It works by allowing investors to defer paying the 18% capital gains tax on investments and instead divert these funds to businesses in designated Opportunity Zones. Any capital gains made on investments in these businesses can also be deferred for up to ten years.

Up to 25% of Washington State can be designated as an Opportunity Zone. Explore the Washington Department of Commerce website to understand which areas in Washington have this designation.

Other tax opportunities

Depending on the type of synergistic relationship pursued, there may be additional tax opportunities to explore. The Washington Department of Revenue lists and explains more than fifty potential tax incentive programs, including:

  • Opportunities for high unemployment counties and Community Empowerment Zones,
  • Biofuel industry producer and manufacturing opportunities,
  • Programs for timber extractors and manufacturers,
  • Sales tax exceptions for anaerobic digesters.

Permits

Permitting is an important part of ensuring your industrial project meets all the criteria for operation. Visit the Department of Ecology website to search for relevant regulations and permits using key words. The most important permits to explore include:

  • Emissions,
  • Stormwater,
  • Noise

Unfortunately, regulation often lags innovation. One such regulation is related to product and part certifications. Parts as small as nuts or bolts that hold products in place may only be approved for a range of uses that doesn’t include synergistic activities. Unauthorized uses may not be allowable for new uses, posing risk for smaller operations. Determine which, if any, of the innovative system need to be certified by a third party.

Commitment to employee growth and retention opportunities is critical for nurturing employees who are dedicated and enthused.. One vital way to invest in employees is to help them build their careers through training opportunities. The following are excellent resources for additional education:

  • Apprenticeships
  • Internships
  • University programs
  • Certifications (e.g. certified plant operators)
  • Unions
